Read through a relativistic electrodynamics lens, the lecture is best seen as a historically important low-energy theory of magnetism rather than a covariant electromagnetic derivation. Van Vleck explicitly identifies the classical-electrodynamics failure: Weiss’s molecular-field picture needs an unphysically large coupling, and a consistent classical statistical treatment makes dia- and paramagnetism cancel, which is essentially the Bohr-van Leeuwen obstruction in modern language. 

What survives the relativistic reading is the field-theory backbone. Maxwell electromagnetism becomes naturally covariant when written in terms of the electromagnetic field tensor and the four-current, and the NIST note emphasizes exactly that unification of electric and magnetic fields under special relativity. Van Vleck’s use of Zeeman shifts, magnetic moments, and g-factors is compatible with that structure, but he keeps the discussion in the condensed-matter language of , , crystal fields, and partition functions rather than in explicit four-vector/tensor form. 

The deepest relativistic correction is on the particle side. Dirac’s 1928 theory is where electron spin and its magnetic moment enter a relativistic quantum description, so Van Vleck’s emphasis on spin, Kramers degeneracy, and Zeeman splitting is physically well placed, but still not a relativistic electrodynamics theory of magnetism. In that sense, the lecture is conceptually strong and empirically successful, while leaving the covariant origin of magnetic moments and the transformation properties of  and  mostly implicit. 

My verdict: excellent physics, but not a relativistic electrodynamics paper in the strict sense. It is a quantum-statistical account of magnetic susceptibility that sits downstream of relativistic field theory, not a derivation from it.
