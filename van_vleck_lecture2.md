Van Vleck’s rare-earth result was mainly about trivalent rare-earth ions in salts/compounds, not about modern NdFeB permanent magnets. His key observation was that the magnetic response of the 4f ions is controlled by a hierarchy of energy scales: the 4f shell is strongly shielded, the crystal field is relatively weak, and for most trivalent rare earths Hund’s first-order picture works well, while Sm³⁺ and Eu³⁺ require the second-order Zeeman term because the relevant multiplet splittings are not large compared with . That is why he found the unexpected low-temperature deviations and the need to sum over nearby levels rather than treat the ion as a single rigid moment. 

Through a modern relativistic electrodynamics lens, this means the magnetism comes from a relativistic electron structure, not from a purely classical dipole. In today’s language, the 4f rare-earth block is dominated by spin–orbit coupling, which scales strongly with atomic number and therefore outruns other magnetic energy scales; the result is that the low-energy states are best described by -multiplets rather than separate spin and orbital pieces. At the field-theory level, electricity and magnetism are components of the same covariant electromagnetic tensor , and the Lorentz force is written as ; what looks like “magnetic” response in one frame is part of the same relativistic interaction in another. 

The vorticity picture is even more intuitive. In macroscopic electrodynamics, magnetization is equivalent to circulating bound currents: the volume current is , and there is also a surface current . So the rare-earth moment is best imagined as a tiny current vortex living inside the ion, with the crystal field deforming that circulation and the spin–orbit coupling locking the orbital shape to the total angular momentum. 

That also clarifies the odd/even-electron contrast Van Vleck emphasized. For odd-electron ions, Kramers degeneracy leaves a low-temperature first-order Zeeman response, so susceptibility can keep its Curie-like  behavior; for even-electron ions, a sufficiently asymmetric crystal field can remove the degeneracy and leave a finite low-temperature susceptibility. In modern terms, this is the difference between a protected magnetic doublet and a state whose response comes mainly from virtual mixing with excited levels. 

So the clean modern translation is: Van Vleck did not uncover “vorticity” in a fluid-mechanics sense, but he did uncover how relativistic electron currents in shielded 4f shells produce robust, anisotropic, and temperature-sensitive magnetism. His work is an early, remarkably accurate low-energy map of a relativistic current-circulation problem hidden inside rare-earth ions.
